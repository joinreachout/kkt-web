Methods

Cost of Doing Nothing — methodology.

This is the methodology behind the KKT Cost of Doing Nothing calculator. The calculator is a single-page interactive tool that takes 6–8 inputs from a fuel-retail operator and returns a quantified, sourced, range-based estimate of the annual P&L gap between current operations and top-quartile peers.

Status v0.1 draft · Calculator launches Phase 1.5

The calculator is in benchmark-calibration. This methodology page is published first — without the methodology document, the calculator would be a gimmick. Per the principles below, this page goes live before any number is computed.

Try the v0 preview tool → Placeholder benchmarks; not for board use.

Why this page exists first

The methodology is the trust mechanism.

The Cost of Doing Nothing tool will produce numbers that go on board slides. Those numbers can be wrong. The single biggest risk is a real CFO catching a math error and posting it to LinkedIn — a risk we take seriously enough that we publish the methodology before the tool itself ships.

Three commitments make this page worth the bytes it occupies: every benchmark in the calculator will trace back to a numbered source listed here; every output will be a range, not a point; and every change to either the calculator or this document will appear in the public changelog at the bottom of this page.

Principles

Seven non-negotiables.

If any one is missing, the calculator stops being a CFO tool and starts being a vendor toy. They are the contract between this page and the user.

  1. 01

    Outputs are ranges, not points.

    Never €2,847,392 lost annually. Always €1.8M–€3.1M annually, depending on which gaps are realistically closable in 18 months. Pretending precision we do not have is the fastest way to lose a CFO.

  2. 02

    Methodology is visible on every output.

    Every computed figure has an expandable How we calculated this panel: source, top-quartile benchmark, user input, gap calculation, date of underlying benchmark data. Nothing in the calculator is unauditable.

  3. 03

    The calculator must sometimes return no significant gap.

    If a top-quartile operator inputs their numbers and still sees you are losing €X — the calculator is broken. There is at least one branch of output for operators already running at top-quartile.

  4. 04

    Sensitivity is shown.

    A panel names the top three inputs that drove the result and approximately how much each contributed. The user can see which assumption is doing the heavy lifting before they trust the number.

  5. 05

    Methodology is public and dated.

    This page. Versioned in lockstep with the calculator. Every revision visible in the changelog. This is what makes the calculator Gartner-grade rather than vendor-grade.

  6. 06

    Conservative-by-default.

    When a calibration choice exists between aggressive and conservative, we choose conservative. We probably underestimate reads as serious. We probably overestimate reads as sales pitch.

  7. 07

    Inputs stay in the user’s browser.

    No server transmission unless the user explicitly chooses to attach their results to a diagnostic booking. Stated visibly in the UI. The whole point is to be useful to a CFO who does not want their numbers leaving their machine.

Sources

Where the benchmark numbers come from.

Every benchmark in the calculator must be traceable to a numbered source in this document. Sources are weighted in the order below — primary filings beat secondary aggregators, association data beats trade press, trade press beats consultancy. KKT internal data sits last because it is also the only data we own; we are explicit about that.

  1. Tier 1

    Public annual reports of listed fuel retailers

    MOL, OMV, Couche-Tard, EG Group, Shell retail, BP retail, ENI retail. Margin per liter, convenience attach rate, working capital days — all disclosed in primary filings. We build the benchmark table from filings, not from secondary aggregators.

  2. Tier 2

    Industry association publications

    UPEI (European fuel suppliers), national fuel-retail associations across CE/EU. Annual benchmark studies. Cited by document and date.

  3. Tier 3

    Trade press surveys

    Fuels Market News, Petrol Plaza, Convenience Store News, NACS Convenience Industry Report. Survey-based but methodology disclosed and traceable.

  4. Tier 4

    Major consultancy whitepapers

    Kearney Fuel Retail Outlook, periodic BCG and McKinsey publications. Cited and linked, never repackaged as our own. We are explicit about what is ours and what is theirs.

  5. Tier 5

    KKT internal aggregated benchmarks

    From real engagements 2022–2026, anonymized, minimum n=5 per benchmark category. This is the moat layer — only KKT has it. Always disclosed as such, with sample size shown.

Each calculator output will cite specific entries — for example: top-quartile shrinkage in CE/EU fuel-retail networks 100–500 stations: 1.4–1.7% (Sources: NACS Convenience Industry Report 2024 §3.2; UPEI European Fuel Retail Survey 2024 Table 7; KKT engagements 2022–2025, n=8).

Inputs and outputs

What the calculator will ask, what it will return.

The calculator will collect 6–8 inputs (number of stations, region, revenue band, non-fuel share, analytical maturity, shrinkage, stockout frequency, working capital days). Half are required; the rest accept "I don't know" — losing half the audience to a mandatory shrinkage-rate field is unacceptable.

The output will arrive in three blocks:

  1. Where you sit on the staged maturity model. A short narrative placement. Honest about what was inferred and from what. Sometimes: "you appear to be operating at top-quartile on these metrics — gap is in other categories."
  2. Three quantified gap scenarios. Conservative / median / aggressive. Each as an annual P&L impact range with a realistic timeline (12 / 18 / 24–36 months). Each cell expands to show the methodology for that specific number.
  3. What would actually unlock these gaps. Three to five Playbook services tagged with realistic effort and timeline, linked to the relevant page. Closing CTA: book a diagnostic — we walk this with your real data.
Risk mitigation

How we keep this honest.

  • Pre-launch external review. An external fuel-retail CFO — not a KKT consultant — stress-tests 20 input combinations specifically to find errors. Paid engagement, scope: find errors.
  • "Spotted an error?" channel. From day one, the UI will carry a visible button to report calculation issues. Every reported issue gets a public response on this page.
  • Lockstep versioning. The calculator and this document share a version number (v1.0, v1.1, v1.2…). Every revision appears in the changelog below.
  • Inputs stay local. We do not transmit user inputs to our servers unless the user explicitly chooses to attach their results to a diagnostic booking.
