A diagnostic is not a strategy deck. It is a fourteen-day
investigation that produces one decision-grade artefact: the readout.
This post is for the executive who is about to receive one — what to
expect inside, what to push back on, and what the document is
deliberately not.

## What is in the readout

Three things, in this order.

**The picture as we see it.** One paragraph each on the operating
reality we found, the data we pulled, the people we talked to. No
posture, no compliments, no setup. If something contradicts the brief
we were given, it appears here.

**What to ship, what to leave alone, what to validate first.** A
sequenced backlog. Each item has effort, impact, and confidence
ranges — never single numbers. Items we are confident about appear
first; items that depend on weak data are flagged and demoted.

**The exit criterion for whichever stage actually matters.** This is
the most important part and the one most often overlooked on first
read. Before recommending an engagement, we name what would have to
be true for the engagement to end. If we cannot name a clean exit,
we do not recommend the engagement.

## What is not in the readout

No vendor recommendations. No tooling list. No five-year roadmap. No
"three pillars of digital transformation". If you got one of those
instead of a readout, ours got lost in transit.

Also no certainty we do not have. A line like "this will increase
margin by 14%" never appears. A line like "based on current data,
top-quartile peers in this segment operate at 1.4-1.7% shrinkage and
your floor estimate is 2.4-2.9%" does.

## What to push back on

Three things deserve hard questions on the readout call.

**Where did this number come from?** Ask about every number in the
ranges. We will name the source — public filing, association
benchmark, our own engagements, your own data. If we cite our own
engagements, ask for the sample size. We are aiming for n equal to
five or more before we will quote a benchmark range.

**What if assumption X is wrong?** The sequenced backlog depends on
some assumptions about your operating reality. The senior people who
will commit to the engagement should test the assumptions explicitly.
If the answer is "the recommendation falls apart", that is useful —
better to find out at the readout than three months in.

**Why is this not stage three?** Most readouts recommend stage one or
two work. Some clients want stage three. The readout will say why we
are recommending what we are. Push back if the why is thin.

## What happens after the readout

The readout is the deliverable. After that, two things are possible:
we agree on a sequenced engagement, or we do not. If we do not, the
readout is yours to act on without us. The senior counterpart inside
your organisation can take the backlog and ship it themselves, or
hand it to a different partner, or shelve it. We do not retain
ownership of the recommendations.

This is on purpose. A readout that only works if KKT runs it is not
a readout — it is a sales motion in a notebook.
